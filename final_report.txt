\begin{abstract}
%% ============================================================

Existing attribution methods for Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) identify
document importance through causal influence on model outputs---typically
measured by log-probability degradation after document removal. We show that
this definition conflates two distinct properties: a document can strongly
influence model confidence while providing no factual benefit, or even pushing
the model toward an incorrect answer. We propose \lodo (Leave-One-Document-Out),
a three-axis evaluation framework that jointly measures factual degradation,
log-probability collapse, and layer-wise representation drift when each retrieved
document is removed. Applying \lodo to the counterfactual RGB benchmark with
Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, using exclusively the counter-factual documents in
\texttt{en\_counter\_mid}, we find that \textbf{98.5\%} of documents flagged as
``important'' under standard likelihood-based attribution cause \emph{zero}
factual degradation---a phenomenon we term \emph{collapse divergence}. We
introduce a six-category factuality-aware importance taxonomy
(factuality-critical, confidence-only, factuality-disrupting, and three
non-influential variants) and show that factuality-aware ranking achieves
P@1\,=\,1.00 versus 0.00 for logprob-only ranking on queries with at least one
factually impacting document. A mechanistic analysis reveals an \emph{Integration
Horizon} at layers 18--20 where retrieved evidence begins to dominate internal
representations, and that log-probability collapse is localized to 1--2 factual
tokens per output. Together, these findings demonstrate that influence-based
attribution must be extended with factuality-aware interpretation to reliably
identify which retrieved documents genuinely affect model factuality.

\end{abstract}


%% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
%% ============================================================

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) grounds language model outputs in
externally retrieved documents, improving factual accuracy in open-domain
question answering and knowledge-intensive tasks~\cite{lewis2020rag}.
A fundamental question for both reliability and interpretability is:
\emph{which of the retrieved documents actually caused the model to produce
the correct answer?}

Existing attribution methods answer this by measuring causal influence.
ContextCite~\cite{cohen2024contextcite} trains a sparse linear surrogate over
context ablations. TracLLM~\cite{wang2025tracllm} applies recursive partitioning
to long contexts. AttriBoT~\cite{dubey2025attribot} approximates leave-one-out
attribution via cached activations. All three methods score a document as
important when its removal changes the model's output distribution---typically
quantified by a drop in log-likelihood of the generated response.

\paragraph{The gap.}
Causal influence on output probability is not the same as factual impact.
A document that confidently asserts a false fact can cause large log-probability
collapse when removed, because the model has ``anchored'' its generation on that
misinformation---yet the correct answer survives unchanged. Conversely, a document
can be necessary for the correct answer without making a large dent in aggregate
log-probability. Attribution methods that rely solely on likelihood degradation
cannot distinguish these cases: they measure \emph{what shifted the model's
output}, not \emph{whether that shift affected factual correctness}.

This distinction is not academic. In a medical or legal RAG system, a misleading
document that scores as ``highly important'' under influence-based attribution
provides genuinely wrong guidance: it suggests the document should be trusted,
when in fact it is the document the system should resist.

\paragraph{This work.}
We propose \textbf{Factuality-Aware \lodo (Leave-One-Document-Out)}, a
three-axis evaluation framework that simultaneously measures:
(i)~\emph{factual degradation} ($\dfact$)---whether removing a document changes
the model's factual correctness;
(ii)~\emph{log-probability collapse} ($\dlogp$)---how much the model's
confidence in its original answer drops; and
(iii)~\emph{representation drift} ($\dltwo$)---how much internal hidden states
shift layer by layer.

Using these three axes, we introduce a \textbf{factuality-aware importance
taxonomy} that separates documents the model genuinely relies on for factual
generation from documents that merely inflate or deflate its confidence.
We quantify the prevalence of \emph{collapse divergence}---cases where
log-probability collapses but factual correctness is unaffected---through
the \textbf{Collapse Divergence Rate} (\cdr).

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
  \item We formalize the distinction between causal influence and factual
        impact in document attribution, and show the two are empirically
        misaligned.
  \item We propose \lodo, a three-axis post-hoc evaluation framework that
        jointly measures factual degradation, likelihood degradation, and
        representation drift per removed document.
  \item We define the Collapse Divergence Rate and show it reaches 98.5\% on
        the pure counterfactual RGB benchmark: virtually all
        likelihood-important documents produce zero factual effect.
  \item We introduce a six-category importance taxonomy and show that
        factuality-aware ranking achieves P@1\,=\,1.00 versus 0.00 for
        logprob-only ranking.
  \item We identify an \emph{Integration Horizon} at transformer layers 18--20
        and show that log-probability collapse is localized to 1--2 factual
        tokens per output, revealing a sparse internal attribution structure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Document Attribution in RAG}

Attribution methods for RAG estimate which context segments contributed to a
specific model output. ContextCite~\cite{cohen2024contextcite} fits a sparse
linear model via LASSO over context-ablation outputs to score each sentence.
TracLLM~\cite{wang2025tracllm} handles long contexts through recursive
partitioning and contribution-score denoising, enabling attribution over
multi-document contexts. AttriBoT~\cite{dubey2025attribot} approximates
leave-one-out attribution efficiently using cached activations and proxy
models, enabling practical attribution at scale.

A shared assumption across these methods is that causal contribution to the
output is the right proxy for document importance. A document is scored as
important if its absence measurably changes the model's output distribution.
This conflates influence with factual impact: a document asserting a confidently
wrong answer can score highly important under all three methods.

\subsection{Mechanistic Interpretability of RAG Evidence}

Prior mechanistic work has examined how transformer models process in-context
information. Attention patterns in early layers encode structural dependencies,
while mid-to-late layers integrate semantic content~\cite{meng2022rome,
geva2021transformer}. In RAG settings, evidence shows that factual knowledge
is stored in and retrieved from mid-layer MLP
modules~\cite{meng2022rome,dai2022knowledge}, but the interaction between
parametric knowledge and retrieved context at the representation level is
less studied. Our layer-wise drift analysis extends this direction to
the specific question of \emph{where} retrieved documents influence generation.

\subsection{Factuality in RAG Evaluation}

Factuality evaluation for RAG has largely focused on end-to-end
metrics~\cite{chen2024rgb,es2023ragas}. The RGB benchmark~\cite{chen2024rgb}
evaluates noise robustness, rejection behavior, and counterfactual resistance
across retrieval conditions. Our work uses RGB's counterfactual subset as the
evaluation setting, which places factual correctness in direct tension with
retrieved evidence---an ideal condition for observing the influence-usefulness
gap.

\section{Problem Formulation}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $q$ be a query and $\mathbf{D} = \{d_1, \dots, d_n\}$ a set of $n$
retrieved documents. A model $\mathcal{M}$ generates a response
$\hat{y} = \mathcal{M}(q, \mathbf{D})$, and a factuality scorer $f$
assigns a binary correctness label $f(\hat{y}, y^*) \in \{0, 1\}$,
where $y^*$ is the ground-truth answer.

\paragraph{LODO ablation.}
For each document $d_i \in \mathbf{D}$, we define the \emph{ablated context}
$\mathbf{D}_{-i} = \mathbf{D} \setminus \{d_i\}$. We generate a new response
$\hat{y}_{-i} = \mathcal{M}(q, \mathbf{D}_{-i})$ and evaluate three axes of
degradation relative to the full-context \emph{baseline}:

\begin{align}
  \dfact(d_i) &= f(\hat{y}, y^*) - f(\hat{y}_{-i}, y^*) \label{eq:dfact}\\
  \dlogp(d_i) &= \log P_{\mathcal{M}}(\hat{y} \mid q, \mathbf{D}_{-i})
                 - \log P_{\mathcal{M}}(\hat{y} \mid q, \mathbf{D}) \label{eq:dlogp}\\
  \dltwo^{(\ell)}(d_i) &=
    \bigl\lVert \bar{h}^{(\ell)}(\mathbf{D}) -
    \bar{h}^{(\ell)}(\mathbf{D}_{-i}) \bigr\rVert_2 \label{eq:dl2}
\end{align}

where $\bar{h}^{(\ell)}$ denotes the mean hidden state at transformer layer
$\ell$ over all output tokens. $\dlogp$ is computed via teacher forcing over
the fixed baseline answer $\hat{y}$, making it fully deterministic and
independent of sampling temperature.

\paragraph{Causal influence vs factual impact.}
Prior attribution methods define document importance as causal influence:
document $d_i$ is important if $|\dlogp(d_i)|$ exceeds a threshold $\thr$.
We argue this definition is incomplete. We instead define:

\begin{definition}[Factuality-Impacting]
  $d_i$ is \emph{factuality-impacting} if $\dfact(d_i) \neq 0$: its removal
  changes the model's factual correctness in either direction.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Logprob-Important]
  $d_i$ is \emph{logprob-important} if $\dlogp(d_i) < -2.0$: its removal
  causes strong log-probability collapse in the original answer.
\end{definition}

A document can be logprob-important without being factuality-impacting (collapse
divergence), and can be factuality-impacting with only a modest logprob drop
(fact-only). The Collapse Divergence Rate formalizes how often
the two concepts disagree:

\begin{equation}
  \cdr = \frac{|\{d_i : \dlogp < -\thr,\ \dfact = 0\}|}
              {|\{d_i : \dlogp < -\thr\}|}
  \label{eq:cdr}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Factuality-aware taxonomy.}
We classify each ablated document into one of six mutually exclusive categories
based on its ($\dlogp$, $\dfact$) pair:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Category & $\dlogp$ & $\dfact$ \\
\midrule
Factuality-critical   & $< -\thr$ & $> 0$ \\
Confidence-only       & $< -\thr$ & $= 0$ \\
Factuality-disrupting & $< -\thr$ & $< 0$ \\
Fact-only             & $\geq -\thr$ & $> 0$ \\
Neutral               & $\geq -\thr$ & $= 0$ \\
Factuality-weak       & $\geq -\thr$ & $< 0$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\emph{Factuality-critical} documents are both causally influential and
factually necessary. \emph{Factuality-disrupting} documents are influential
and actively push the model toward incorrect answers.
\emph{Confidence-only} documents are the CDR population:
influential by the likelihood criterion but factually irrelevant.


%% ============================================================
\section{Method: Factuality-Aware LODO}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{LODO Procedure}

For each query $q$ with retrieved documents $\mathbf{D}$:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Generate the baseline answer $\hat{y}$ from $\mathcal{M}(q, \mathbf{D})$
        and record its factuality score $f(\hat{y}, y^*)$ and log-probability.
  \item For each document $d_i \in \mathbf{D}$:
        \begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
          \item Generate a new answer $\hat{y}_{-i}$ from $\mathcal{M}(q,
                \mathbf{D}_{-i})$ and compute $\dfact(d_i)$.
          \item Compute $\dlogp(d_i)$ via teacher-forcing $\hat{y}$ under the
                ablated context (deterministic; no sampling required).
          \item Compute $\dltwo^{(\ell)}(d_i)$ at selected layers $\ell$.
        \end{enumerate}
  \item Classify each $d_i$ into the factuality-aware taxonomy using
        threshold $\thr = 2.0$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Factuality-Aware Importance Score}

In addition to the binary taxonomy, we define a \emph{factuality-aware causal
score} for continuous ranking:
\begin{equation}
  s_{\mathrm{FA}}(d_i) = \max(0,\,-\dlogp(d_i)) \cdot \max(0,\,\dfact(d_i))
  \label{eq:fa_score}
\end{equation}

This score is non-zero only when a document is both logprob-important
\emph{and} factuality-critical, directly operationalizing our definition of
document importance.

\subsection{Layer-Wise and Token-Wise Analysis}

\paragraph{Integration Horizon.}
We track $\dltwo^{(\ell)}$ across all transformer layers. Prior work
suggests factual grounding occurs in mid-to-late layers; we test whether
documents classified as factuality-critical vs confidence-only produce
distinguishable layer-wise drift profiles.

\paragraph{Fact-token logprob.}
For each output token $t_j$, we compute the per-token logprob drop
$\delta_j = \log p(t_j | t_{<j}, \mathbf{D}_{-i}) - \log p(t_j | t_{<j},
\mathbf{D})$. We classify tokens as \emph{factual} (digits, dates, capitalized
proper-noun tokens) or \emph{structural} (prepositions, function words) and
compare the concentration of logprob collapse across token types.

\section{Experimental Setup}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Dataset.}
We use the \textbf{en\_counter\_mid} subset of the RGB
benchmark~\cite{chen2024rgb}. In this subset, the retrieved documents are
relevantly modified to assert an answer that contradicts the ground truth.
Crucially, the \texttt{positive} field of each instance contains these
counter-factual documents; we use \emph{only} these counter-factual documents
to construct the retrieved context, ensuring a pure counterfactual robustness
setting in which the model must \emph{resist} all retrieved evidence to produce
the correct answer.

\paragraph{Why not en\_mid?}
The standard \texttt{en\_mid} subset has near-ceiling accuracy (92--96\%)
regardless of which documents are present. In this regime, LODO is causally
uninformative: factual degradation is unobservable because removing any
single document rarely flips the model's (already correct) answer.
\texttt{en\_counter\_mid} reduces accuracy to 0--10\% depending on passage
count (Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_motivation}), creating a setting where document
presence is causally observable.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/CF4_dataset_motivation.png}
  \caption{Accuracy on \texttt{en\_mid} vs.\ \texttt{en\_counter\_mid}.
           Near-ceiling accuracy on \texttt{en\_mid} makes document removal a
           non-event. \texttt{en\_counter\_mid} (pure counter-factual) places
           baseline accuracy at 0--10\%, making individual document presence
           causally observable.}
  \label{fig:dataset_motivation}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Model.}
All experiments use \textbf{Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct} ($\tau = 0.7$ for
generation; teacher-forcing for $\dlogp$ and $\dltwo$).

\paragraph{Passage-number sweep.}
We run \lodo with passage\_num $\in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$ on 10 queries from
\texttt{en\_counter\_mid}, holding the query set and random seed (42) fixed.
For each passage\_num, we sample $\min(\text{passage\_num},\, |\text{counter-factual docs}|)$
documents from the \texttt{positive} (counter-factual) field, so that all
retrieved documents assert the wrong answer.

\paragraph{Scale.}
The sweep produces 181 ablations across all passage counts
($28 + 40 + 50 + 63$ ablations for $n \in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$).
We additionally run \lodo on 10 queries from \texttt{en\_refine} (all with
correct baselines), producing 275 ablations, for a total of 456 ablations
in the full analysis table.

\section{Results}
%% ============================================================

We organize results around five research questions that each probe one
axis of the influence-usefulness gap.

\subsection{RQ1: Does Likelihood-Based Importance Overestimate Factual Impact?}

\paragraph{Collapse Divergence Rate.}
Across the 181 sweep ablations, 67 documents (37.0\%) satisfy the standard
logprob-importance criterion ($\dlogp < -2.0$). Among these, \textbf{66
documents (98.5\%) cause zero factual change}---they are classified as
confidence-only. Zero are factuality-critical and only 1 is
factuality-disrupting (1.5\%). The resulting \cdr = 98.5\% (Table~\ref{tab:cdr}).

\bottomrule

\paragraph{Finding.}
The \cdr is remarkably stable: it stays between 95.7\% and 100.0\% across all
passage counts (Figure~\ref{fig:cdr_by_pn}). This rules out the hypothesis
that collapse divergence is a small-$n$ artifact. At every level of redundancy,
the model's internal confidence collapses frequently in response to document
removal without any corresponding change in factual correctness.

\subsection{RQ2: Can Factuality-Aware Scoring Separate Documents by Factuality Impact?}

\paragraph{Importance taxonomy.}
Across all 456 ablations, document importance decomposes as shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy_stacked}: 83.1\% are \emph{neutral} (no effect on
either axis), 15.1\% are \emph{confidence-only}, 0.4\% are \emph{fact-only}

\emph{factuality-critical} (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}).

A structurally important asymmetry emerges: factuality-critical cases cannot
appear when the baseline accuracy is 0\%---factual degradation requires a

neutral, fact-only, and factuality-weak. Only at $n=10$, where 10\% of baselines
are correct, do we observe a single fact-only case (factual change without logprob
collapse).

MRR\,=\,0.33. The Spearman correlation with $\dfact$ is 0.50 for factuality-aware
scoring versus 0.02 for logprob-only.

\paragraph{A single regime.}
In the pure counterfactual setting, the model is in a \emph{context dominance}
regime across all passage counts: baseline accuracy is 0\% for $n \leq 7$ and
only 10\% at $n=10$. This means $\dfact$ can only take the value 0 or $-1$
(removal helps the model), and factuality-critical cases ($\dfact > 0$) are
structurally impossible when the baseline is incorrect. The CDR remains at
or above 95\% at every $n$, confirming that the influence-usefulness gap is
not a low-redundancy artifact but a fundamental property of the counterfactual
setting.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
Adding more retrieved counter-factual documents does not resolve the
influence-usefulness gap. Even at $n=10$, virtually all logprob-important
documents are confidence-only. The only factuality-impacting case at $n=10$
appears in the fact-only category (small logprob drop, $\dfact \neq 0$),
which logprob-only attribution would miss entirely.

\subsection{RQ4: Where Inside the Model Does Evidence Integration Occur?}

\paragraph{Integration Horizon.}
Layer-wise drift trajectories from detailed case studies (10 ablations with
full 33-layer coverage) reveal a consistent structural pattern
(Figure~\ref{fig:layerwise}): drift is near zero through layers 0--17,
then rises sharply at layers 18--20. We term this the
\textbf{Integration Horizon}---the transition point where syntactic/structural
encoding gives way to factual grounding in retrieved context.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{results/factuality_aware_extensions_20260430_004226/plots/E4_full_33layer_drift_case_studies.png}
  \caption{Full 33-layer L2 drift trajectories from detailed case studies.
           Gray: average-baseline cases ($\dlogp \approx 0$).
           Red: top-divergence cases ($\dlogp < -2$).
           The Integration Horizon at layers 18--20 is visible in both groups,
           but magnitude differs substantially.}
  \label{fig:layerwise}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Mechanistic taxonomy comparison.}
Using three-checkpoint drift (layers 0, 16, 32) across all sweep ablations,
we compare mean L2 drift at layer 32 by taxonomy category
(Table~\ref{tab:layer_drift}). Confidence-only documents (15.61) produce an
order-of-magnitude larger drift than neutral documents (5.90). The single
factuality-disrupting document (12.90) shows comparable drift to confidence-only,
confirming that high representation disruption is a feature of logprob-important
documents regardless of their factual direction.

           All logprob-important categories (confidence-only, factuality-disrupting)
           show sharp late-layer increases while neutral documents remain near
           zero throughout.}
  \label{fig:layerwise_cat}
\end{figure}

This finding has an important implication: representation drift---like logprob
degradation---measures the \emph{magnitude} of a document's influence on the
model, not its \emph{direction}. Factuality-aware attribution requires
$\dfact$ to provide the directionality that neither $\dlogp$ nor $\dltwo$
can supply alone.

\subsection{RQ5: Is Causal Influence Localized to Factual Tokens?}

Token-level logprob analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:token}) on 10 detailed case
studies reveals that log-probability collapse is concentrated on factual tokens
(named entities, numbers, dates), which comprise 48\% of output tokens by count.
Structural tokens (prepositions, conjunctions, determiners) exhibit minimal
change under document ablation.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{results/factuality_aware_extensions_20260430_004226/plots/E3_token_type_logprob_drop_boxplot.png}
  \caption{Per-token logprob change under document ablation, for top-divergence
           (left) and average-baseline (right) cases. Factual tokens (orange)
           show substantially larger negative shifts than structural tokens
           (blue) in top-divergence cases; both are near-zero in average-baseline
           cases. Token types identified via heuristics: digit-containing tokens
           and capitalized tokens with $|t| > 1$.}
  \label{fig:token}
\end{figure}

This token-level sparsity parallels the document-level sparsity: causal
influence in RAG is doubly concentrated---in a small fraction of documents,
and within those documents' effects, in a small fraction of output tokens.
The implication is that fact-token logprob drop may be a tighter predictor
of factual impact than whole-sequence logprob drop, though confirming
this quantitatively requires token-level data at larger scale than the 10
case studies analyzed here (see Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).


%% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Why Influence Is Not Usefulness}

The central finding of this paper---CDR $\approx$ 98.5\% across all
conditions---reflects a fundamental property of the pure counterfactual setting:
in \texttt{en\_counter\_mid}, all retrieved documents assert an incorrect answer.
When the model encounters only counter-factual documents, it is almost never
able to produce the correct answer (0\% baseline accuracy at $n \leq 7$, 10\%
at $n=10$). When a counter-factual document is removed, the model's confidence
in its (incorrect) answer may shift substantially---but factual correctness rarely
changes because the baseline was already wrong. This is influence without
factual impact.

Existing attribution methods (ContextCite, TracLLM, AttriBoT) would score such
a document as highly important. Our factuality-aware framework identifies it
as confidence-only. The practical consequence is significant: a confidence-only
document should \emph{not} be cited as the cause of an incorrect output, even
if standard attribution ranks it first.

\subsection{The Role of Baseline Accuracy}

A key structural finding is that factuality-critical cases require a correct
baseline to exist: $\dfact > 0$ implies $f(\hat{y}, y^*) = 1$. In the pure
counterfactual setting, this condition is almost never satisfied. This is not
a weakness of the LODO method---it is an informative finding: when all retrieved
documents push toward the wrong answer, the model's internal parametric knowledge
is insufficient to produce correct answers in most cases. The CDR of nearly 100\%
is therefore both a measurement of attribution failure and a measurement of
counter-factual robustness failure.

\subsection{Implications for Attribution Methods}

We do not claim that likelihood-based attribution is wrong. It correctly
identifies which documents shift the model's output distribution. Our claim is
narrower: likelihood shift is an incomplete criterion for identifying
\emph{factuality-impacting} documents. Attribution methods that rank documents
by $|\dlogp|$ should be extended with a factuality filter---either through
post-hoc correction (as in our factuality-aware score, Eq.~\ref{eq:fa_score})
or by modifying the attribution objective to weight likelihood changes by
factual alignment.

\subsection{Implications for RAG Evaluation}

Our results suggest that RAG evaluation metrics should track not just aggregate
accuracy but also the proportion of causally important documents that are
factuality-impacting. A RAG system with low CDR (most influential documents
actually affect factuality) is more reliable than one with high CDR (most
influential documents are confidence-only noise). The \lodo framework can serve
as a diagnostic tool for auditing retrieval corpus quality and retriever behavior.

\subsection{Future Directions}

The most immediate extension is factuality-aware reranking: use \lodo-derived
labels to fine-tune the retriever or reranker toward selecting documents that
are factuality-impacting rather than merely confidence-shifting. The integration
horizon finding (Section 6.4) suggests that mid-layer representations---specifically
around layers 18--20---could serve as a real-time signal to flag generation
episodes where retrieved context is having strong but factually irrelevant
effects, potentially before decoding completes.


%% ============================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Post-hoc and computationally expensive.}
\lodo requires $n$ additional forward passes per query (one per ablated
document), plus factuality evaluation of each generated answer.
At inference time, factuality labels are unavailable before generation, so
\lodo cannot be used for online retrieval decisions without an oracle. It is
best suited as a diagnostic and evaluation tool.

\paragraph{Factuality evaluator quality.}
The $\dfact$ signal depends on the quality of the factuality scorer $f$.
We use exact-match and keyword-match evaluation from the RGB benchmark,
which may underestimate factual impact for semantically equivalent
paraphrases. A stronger entailment-based evaluator could increase the
number of observed factuality-impacting cases.

\paragraph{Pure counterfactual setting.}
All sweep experiments use \texttt{en\_counter\_mid} with exclusively
counter-factual documents. Baseline accuracy is near zero, which structurally
prevents factuality-critical cases from appearing. The CDR near 100\% partly
reflects this constraint. Evaluating \lodo in mixed or non-adversarial retrieval
settings---where the model can produce correct baselines---is an important
next step to observe the full taxonomy.

\paragraph{Ranking evaluation is limited to one query.}
The factuality-aware ranking comparison (RQ2) is based on a single non-trivial
query (one query with $\dfact \neq 0$). While the direction of the result is
consistent with the CDR finding, single-query ranking evaluation is insufficient
for robust conclusions. Scaling to settings with higher baseline accuracy would
yield more factuality-impacting documents and a more informative ranking evaluation.

\paragraph{Token-level analysis is limited.}
The fact-token logprob analysis (Section 6.5) is based on 10 case studies
selected for extreme logprob divergence. With only 10 observations---none of
which exhibit $\dfact \neq 0$---we cannot quantify whether fact-token logprob
predicts factual impact better than whole-sequence logprob. Scaling this
analysis to all ablations requires re-running inference with token-level output
recording, which is deferred to future work.

\paragraph{Positional encoding confound.}
Standard \lodo removes document $d_i$ and shifts all subsequent document
positions, changing their RoPE encodings. We have implemented
position-controlled ablation (replacing removed documents with length-matched
neutral placeholders) but have not yet run it at scale due to compute
constraints. The Integration Horizon at layers 18--20 may partially reflect
positional re-encoding rather than semantic absence; this should be controlled
in future work.

\paragraph{Single model and dataset.}
All results use Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on a single benchmark. Generalizability
to other model families, scales, and retrieval settings requires broader
evaluation.


%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We have argued and empirically demonstrated that causal influence on output
probability is an incomplete criterion for document importance in RAG.
The core evidence is a Collapse Divergence Rate of 98.5\%: among all
documents that cause strong log-probability collapse under standard attribution
criteria, virtually all cause zero factual change. The model's internal
confidence can be substantially disrupted by a counter-factual document that
plays no role in whether the answer is correct or incorrect.

We proposed Factuality-Aware \lodo, a three-axis evaluation framework that
jointly measures factual change, likelihood degradation, and representation
drift per removed document. The resulting six-category importance taxonomy
reveals that factuality-impacting documents are extremely rare in the pure
counterfactual setting, and that confidence-only documents---the source of
attribution error---are the dominant non-neutral category at every passage count.

Mechanistically, evidence integration in Llama-3.1-8B is concentrated at a
late Integration Horizon (layers 18--20), and logprob collapse is localized to
1--2 factual tokens per output. Confidence-only and factuality-disrupting
documents produce similar magnitudes of internal disruption---confirming that
representation drift, like likelihood degradation, is directionally agnostic.

Together, these findings motivate a principled extension of RAG attribution:
not abandoning influence-based methods, but supplementing them with factuality
alignment to distinguish documents that genuinely affect model factuality
from those that merely alter its confidence.
